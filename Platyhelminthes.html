Phylum Coelenterata (Cnidaria)

Introduction

  • Aquatic animals, mostly marine; some freshwater (e.g., Hydra)
  • Diploblastic → body made of two layers
  • Radial symmetry → parts arranged around central axis

Body Structure

  • Body wall: Epidermis (outer), Gastrodermis (inner), Mesoglea (jelly layer)
  • Single cavity: Coelenteron (gastrovascular cavity)
  • Mouth is the only opening (functions as mouth + anus)
  • Tentacles around mouth help in locomotion & food capture

Specialized Cells

  • Cnidocytes → stinging cells with nematocysts (defense & prey capture)
  • Nerve cells → simple nerve net
  • Epitheliomuscular cells → contraction & movement
  • Interstitial cells → regeneration

Key Features

  • Tissue-level organization, no true organs
  • Two body forms:
    • Polyp → sessile, cylindrical (Hydra, Corals)
    • Medusa → umbrella-shaped, free-swimming (Jellyfish)
  • Incomplete alimentary canal
  • Respiration through body surface

Subphyla

  • Anthozoa → only polyp stage (Corals, Sea anemones)
  • Medusozoa → polyp + medusa stages (Hydra, Obelia, Aurelia, Box jellyfish)

Classes

  • Hydrozoa → Hydra, Obelia (polyp + medusa)
  • Scyphozoa → True jellyfish (Aurelia), medusa dominant
  • Anthozoa → Corals, Sea anemones (polyp only)
  • Cubozoa → Box jellyfish, cube-shaped, highly venomous

Reproduction

  • Asexual → budding, regeneration (Hydra, Corals)
  • Sexual → gametes → planula larva → polyp
  • Alternation of generations: Polyp ↔ Medusa

Importance

  • Coral reefs → biodiversity, coastal protection, tourism
  • Food chain → eaten by fishes, turtles
  • Jellyfish stings can be harmful
  • Corals → ecological and aesthetic value
